ABSTRACT With the widespread application of Unmanned Surface Vehicles (USVs) in marine monitoring
and military reconnaissance, collaborative pursuit in complex and dynamic environments faces significant
challenges. Traditional path planning methods (e.g., A∗ algorithm, artificial potential field) suffer from
limited adaptability to dynamic obstacles and difficulty in multi-objective collaborative optimization,
while existing deep reinforcement learning approaches often neglect partial observability and variabledimensional observations, resulting in insufficient strategy generalization. To address these challenges,
this paper proposes a POSG-based deep reinforcement learning framework for multi-USV pursuit–evasion
under partial observability, in which an enhanced Multi-Agent Soft Actor-Critic (MASAC) algorithm serves
as the core learning component. By constructing hybrid observation spaces and a hierarchical reward
function, the framework guides pursuer USVs to efficiently approach the target and form stable encirclement
formations, while forcing the evader to adjust its escape trajectory in real time to delay capture. Through
dynamic feature-compression encoding and obstacle-interaction pattern modeling, the proposed method
alleviates the generalization challenge of policy networks in partially observable scenarios with varying
input dimensions. Experimental results show that the proposed framework reaches stable convergence after
approximately 3000 training episodes and achieves an 85% capture success rate over 100 evaluation episodes
in unseen multi-obstacle maps, indicating improved capture efficiency and strong generalization under partial
observability.

formulation in differential game theory that has long served
as a fundamental benchmark for cooperative control in
multi-agent systems. It involves dynamic and adversarial
interactions in which pursuers coordinate to capture targets,
while evaders perform agile evasive maneuvers to escape.
Unmanned Surface Vessels (USVs), owing to their high
mobility and scalability, are well-suited for PEG tasks in

II. RELATED WORK

Early PEG solutions largely rest on analytical control
theories. Isaacs [1] derived optimal control strategies using
variational methods under assumptions of full-state information and (often) linear dynamics. These assumptions
are rarely satisfied in real-world USV applications, where
the dynamics are typically nonlinear, nonholonomic, and
only partially observable. Subsequent research extended
PEG to multi-agent settings [2] and utilized Hamilton–
Jacobi–Isaacs (HJI) equations [3] as well as reachability
analysis in three-dimensional spaces [4]. Nevertheless, these
methods remain restricted to simplified scenarios, suffer
from high computational costs, and require accurate environmental modeling, limiting their applicability in dynamic
maritime environments. In parallel, performance-guaranteed
and constraint-aware optimal control has been actively
studied for multi-agent and USV systems. For example,

J. Liu et al.: DRL-Based Pursuit–Evasion Strategy for USVs in Complex Multi-Obstacle Environments

Cao et al. [5] investigated optimal fixed-time control for
human-in-the-loop multi-agent systems with actuator faults,
providing fault-tolerant coordination with fixed-time convergence. Moreover, Cao et al. [6] proposed a prescribed
performance-based optimal formation control framework
for USVs with position constraints and yaw-angle timevarying partial constraints, highlighting principled handling
of safety/constraint requirements in maritime scenarios.
To address these limitations, reinforcement learning
(RL) has emerged as a promising data-driven approach
for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. Unlike
supervised learning, RL does not rely on labeled datasets
but instead learns effective behaviors through interaction
with the environment. Several studies have applied RL
to pursuit–evasion problems. For instance, Wang et al. [7]
used Q-learning combined with Apollonius-based geometric
reasoning to derive (near-)Nash strategies. Fan et al. [8]
proposed a deep RL framework incorporating noise-injected
neural networks and prioritized replay to achieve real-time
collision avoidance for USVs. However, these approaches
often depend on handcrafted features and may not scale well
in high-dimensional or unstructured environments.
The fusion of deep learning with RL has led to the advent
of Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), which enables
scalable policy learning in complex, partially observable settings. Wang et al. [9] leveraged a Deep Deterministic Policy
Gradient (DDPG) approach for USV containment, achieving
enhanced stability in evasion strategies. Concurrently, MARL
has emerged as a robust framework for modeling systems
comprising multiple autonomous agents. By enabling agents
to learn cooperative or competitive behaviors simultaneously,
MARL facilitates more realistic and scalable solutions.
Concurrently, substantial progress has been achieved
in integrating deep learning with MARL to tackle complex multi-agent decision-making challenges. Specifically,
algorithmic innovations such as value decomposition networks, actor–critic architectures tailored for cooperative
settings, and credit assignment mechanisms have significantly enhanced performance in high-dimensional, uncertain
environments [10], [11], [12]. These advancements lay a
strong theoretical and algorithmic groundwork for applying
MARL to USV-based pursuit–evasion scenarios.
Recent advancements in MARL have demonstrated significant potential for USV applications. Zhang et al. [13]
developed a MADDPG-based multi-UAV pursuit strategy
that emulates human prediction via target estimation networks. Li et al. [14] proposed a minimax-enhanced MADDPG algorithm to enhance generalization against adaptive
adversaries. Focusing on maritime contexts, Qu et al. [15]
presented a DRL-based PEG framework that integrates
PPO with imitation learning for collaborative pursuit and
evasion in multi-obstacle environments. Furthermore, several
recent studies have tackled related challenges in USV
navigation and coordination by leveraging RL techniques.
For instance, a DRL-VO-based method was proposed for

human-like obstacle avoidance [16], while a double DQNbased planner was developed for global path planning in
amphibious USVs [17]. Moreover, a distributed PEG strategy
for perception-limited USV swarms was implemented via
multi-agent PPO [18]. These contributions underscore the
increasing prominence of DRL in maritime autonomous
control.

IV. PROBLEM FORMULATION AND MODELING
A. DESCRIPTION OF PURSUIT-ESCAPE GAME PROBLEMS

For the multi-agent collaborative target capture problem in
unknown environments, this study addresses scenarios with
unknown target trajectories and obstacle interference. The
pursuer swarm dynamically predicts target escape paths and
optimizes containment trajectories and formation patterns in
real-time, leveraging local perceptual data and collaborative
communication mechanisms. By establishing a pursuitevasion differential game framework, the system achieves
multi-agent coordinated blockade and progressive compression of the target’s operational domain, while satisfying USV
kinematic constraints, environmental boundary limitations,
and collision avoidance requirements. The objective is to
effectively complete the containment operation against the
evader USV within specified time thresholds. Simultaneously, the evader USV continuously optimizes its escape
trajectory based on the pursuers’ containment paths and
formation configurations to evade encirclement or maximally
delay capture.
Underlying mechanism (how the proposed method
addresses the concerned problem): The core difficulty
of the considered task is not merely to ‘‘reach’’ the
evader, but to enforce a geometric closure under partial
observability and unknown obstacle interference. To make
this mechanism explicit, we model the interaction as
a multi-pursuer single-evader zero-sum differential game
and implement it as a Partially Observable Stochastic
Game (POSG). Mechanistically, (i) the differential-game
formulation couples the pursuers’ objective (containment)
and the evader’s objective (escape) into a single adversarial
criterion, which prevents the learned policy from exploiting
non-adversarial behaviors; (ii) the POSG observation design
encodes the minimal local geometric information needed
for coordinated encirclement (relative bearings, relative
distances, adjacency-defined capture angles, and obstacle
proximity from beam sensors), thus turning the global
closure requirement into locally computable signals; and
(iii) the hierarchical reward decomposes containment into
complementary sub-mechanisms—fast approach, arrival
synchronization, collision avoidance, and phase-balanced
VOLUME 14, 2026

angle regulation—so that the emergent team behavior
becomes ‘‘blockade-and-compress’’ rather than greedy
interception.

Compared with conventional actor–critic methods, the
Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) paradigm optimizes an entropyregularized objective, which maintains a stochastic policy
and encourages exploration while learning. As an offpolicy actor–critic method, SAC can reuse past transitions
through experience replay, leading to improved sample
efficiency and more stable learning in continuous action
spaces. These properties are particularly beneficial for
multi-USV pursuit–evasion in obstacle-rich environments,
where informative interactions (e.g., near-collision and nearcapture events) may be sparse and safe exploration is
crucial. On the other hand, SAC/MASAC may introduce
additional computational overhead (e.g., multiple critics and
entropy tuning) and can be sensitive to reward scaling and
hyperparameter choices; moreover, in multi-agent adversarial
settings, non-stationarity may still affect training stability.
In this work, we mitigate these issues via centralized training
with decentralized execution, target networks (Polyak aver9924

aging), prioritized experience replay, and alternating updates
between pursuer and evader policies.
For clarity, we briefly summarize the underlying optimization principle of SAC/MASAC used in this work. Our
method follows the maximum-entropy actor–critic objective,
where the policy is trained to maximize the expected return
while maintaining sufficient stochasticity via an entropy
term. Accordingly, the critic is optimized by minimizing the
soft Bellman residual with a target that includes both the nextstep Q-value and the entropy term, while the actor is updated
by minimizing the KL-equivalent objective that trades
off the expected Q-value and the log-likelihood (entropyregularized) term. The detailed derivations of these standard
updates can be found in the original SAC literature [19], and
our implementation adopts this principle within the proposed
POSG-based multi-agent setting.

Underlying mechanism (problem-driven view): The containment task studied in this paper is challenging because

A. PRIORITY EXPERIENCE REPLAY MECHANISM

Traditional reinforcement learning methods typically employ
a uniform sampling strategy in their experience replay (ER)
mechanism during training. However, this approach exhibits
limited performance in complex tasks, particularly due to
insufficient utilization of critical experiences and undersampled high-error transitions. To address this limitation,
Prioritized Experience Replay (PER) enhances sample efficiency and learning speed by assigning sampling priorities
proportional to temporal difference (TD) errors, thereby
prioritizing high-error experiences. To improve sample

quality and convergence performance, this work introduces
a prioritized replay buffer to optimize the experience replay
mechanism in the Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) framework.
Mechanism relevance to the concerned problem: In cooperative containment with obstacles and an adaptive evader,
the transitions that determine success are often sparse and
highly informative (e.g., a brief opening in the formation,
a near-miss collision, or a sudden evader maneuver). Uniform
replay tends to under-sample such ‘‘decision points’’, leading
to slow acquisition of closure behaviors and unstable safety
performance. PER addresses this by biasing sampling toward
large TD-error transitions, which typically correspond to
unexpected outcomes under partial observability; thus the
agents learn faster to correct formation-breaking actions and
to reinforce closure-preserving responses.

Mechanism relevance to the concerned problem: Geometric closure depends on relative configuration (neighbors and
angles), not on the absolute ordering or the exact number

and ‘‘how the formation looks’’ from a variable number

implements this by shared-weight item encoders (homogeneous processing) and pooling-based compression (set
aggregation), allowing the same policy to generalize across
different swarm sizes while preserving the key coordination
cues.

the network of pursuers and evaders is alternately trained
by using the priority experience playback strategy. After
2000 rounds, it is judged whether the pursuer cluster has
completed the round up task. If it is completed, the evader
network is trained, otherwise the pursuer network is trained.

To discuss the reasons behind the convergence trend in

and the adopted training scheme. The pursuer-side distance
reward in Eq. (20) is dense and provides informative gradients
in most states, enabling the policy to rapidly learn a coarse
distance-closing behavior, which yields a fast increase of
returns in early training. Meanwhile, the exponential term
in Eq. (20) introduces a team-level synchronization pressure
by penalizing agents whose remaining capture distance is
larger than the team average, thereby reducing ‘‘one-agentlagging’’ cases and gradually stabilizing the group behavior.
After this stage, further performance improvement mainly
depends on learning the higher-order geometric coordination
required by the angle reward in Eq. (21) (i.e., maintaining
appropriate adjacent capture angles), as well as satisfying
the safety constraints induced by the collision penalty in
Eq. (19); these objectives are harder and improve more
slowly, so the overall return curve tends to plateau after
sufficient training (around 3000 episodes). In addition,

D. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS WITH RELATED METHODS

To further evaluate the performance superiority of the
proposed algorithm, we conducted comparative experiments
with three updated and representative multi-agent reinforcement learning methods: MADDPG (centralized training with
decentralized execution), MAPPO (multi-agent PPO), and
MATD3 (multi-agent TD3). All baseline methods were
trained in the same simulation environment with identical
reward functions and constraints. We evaluated the methods
based on Capture Success Rate (percentage of successful
containment), Average Steps (time efficiency in successful
episodes), Collision Rate (percentage of episodes ending in
obstacle/boundary collisions), and Average Return.
Overall, the proposed MASAC-based framework achieves
a high capture success rate while maintaining a low collision
risk in obstacle-rich environments. The entropy-regularized
objective in MASAC encourages sufficient exploration and
helps avoid premature convergence to unsafe or suboptimal
behaviors (e.g., getting trapped near obstacles or local
minima). Moreover, the off-policy training with experience
replay improves sample efficiency by repeatedly leveraging
informative transitions, including rare but safety-critical
boundary-interaction experiences. These observations indicate that our method effectively balances aggressive pursuit
and safety constraints, leading to stable containment performance in the considered USV pursuit–evasion scenarios.
The aforementioned simulation experiments validate the
improved MASAC algorithm’s local path planning capability
in specific unknown environments. However, unknown
environments inherently exhibit structural diversity, where
environmental parameters such as obstacle distributions and
aquatic boundaries may vary significantly across different
marine regions. If the algorithm is solely optimized for
a specific environmental configuration, its robustness in
heterogeneous unknown scenarios cannot be guaranteed.
To verify whether the local path planning algorithm
truly possesses environment-agnostic autonomous decisionmaking capability, cross-scenario generalization tests were
conducted: the trained policy model was directly transferred

Reason analysis for generalization results (Fig. 14–
Fig. 15). The 85% capture success rate in unseen maps
suggests that the learned pursuer policies capture transferable
interaction patterns rather than memorizing a single obstacle
layout. Successful cases usually occur when the environment
still allows the pursuers to (i) maintain continuous pressure
on the evader with feasible collision-free paths and (ii)
re-form an encirclement after obstacle-induced splitting.
In these cases, even if some agents detour, the team can
recover synchronized closing (Eq. (20)) and restore the
angular configuration encouraged by Eq. (21). In contrast, the
failure cases in Fig. 15 typically arise when obstacles create
narrow passages or multiple branching escape corridors that
amplify desynchronization: different pursuers are forced into
substantially different detours or must significantly reduce
speed for safe navigation, which increases the dispersion
of remaining capture distances (affecting d̄ and σ (d) in
Eq. (20)) and delays the satisfaction of the encirclement
angles required by Eq. (21). The evader, whose objective
is coupled through Eq. (22), can exploit these ‘‘formation
break’’ windows by steering toward such topology-induced
choke points to maximize pursuer coordination errors and
collision risks. This explains why the method generalizes well
in most cases but still fails in a subset of maps with strong
topology-induced desynchronization.

proposed framework, we will extend the experimental study
along two complementary directions. First, we will conduct a
comprehensive benchmark evaluation against representative

VII. CONCLUSION

This paper studies multi-USV pursuit–evasion in unknown
multi-obstacle environments under partial observability.
We formulate the task as a POSG and propose an
enhanced MASAC-based framework integrating hybrid
observations, hierarchical rewards, dynamic feature compression (FEB/OAB), and temporal fusion via masked selfattention with interval-based temporal sampling. Simulation
results show stable convergence and an 85% capture success
rate on 100 unseen-map test episodes.
Limitations: (1) We adopt a simplified 3-DOF planar
kinematic model and do not incorporate hydrodynamic
disturbances (currents/wind/waves), which may cause a simto-real gap. (2) Obstacles are modeled as static circular
regions within an episode, and the game is formulated as a
multi-pursuer single-evader zero-sum capture task. (3) The
current experiments validate a small homogeneous pursuer
group (three USVs), and scalability to larger/heterogeneous
swarms is not yet verified. (4) Additional baseline comparisons and ablation studies are not included in the current
version and are planned in future work.
Future work will focus on: (1) extending to large-scale
and heterogeneous swarms; (2) incorporating hydrodynamic
and actuator dynamics (including latency/saturation); and (3)
expanding to multi-evader and non-zero-sum objectives.
